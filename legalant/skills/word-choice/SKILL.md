# Word Choice — Modal Verb Taxonomy

**Apply to every contract review, redline analysis, and drafting task.**

---

## Modal Verb Hierarchy

| Verb | Legal Force | Usage | Risk on Change |
|------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| **SHALL** | Mandatory obligation. Non-compliance = breach. | Enforceable duties only. | Downgrade to WILL = automatic Substantive flag |
| **WILL** | Statement of intent / future action. Weaker enforceability. | Future commitments, representations. | Upgrade to SHALL = improves enforceability |
| **WOULD** | Conditional or hypothetical. | NEVER in operative clauses. | Flag immediately — replace with SHALL or WILL |
| **MAY** | Discretion or permission. | Rights, options, permissions. | Downgrade from SHALL = weakens obligation |
| **COULD** | Possibility. | NEVER in operative clauses. | Flag immediately — ambiguous enforceability |
| **MUST** | Equivalent to SHALL in modern drafting. | Use SHALL for consistency in Indian contracts. | Acceptable if consistent throughout |

---

## Golden Rule

> Any change between these verbs across contract versions is **automatically a Substantive change**, regardless of context.

**Flag every instance. State:**
1. The original verb
2. The replacement verb
3. The legal consequence of the change
4. The recommended correction

---

## Common Traps

- **"The Company will pay"** — does "will" mean a promise or intention? Use SHALL if it's a duty.
- **"Either party may terminate"** — discretionary; neither party has an obligation to terminate.
- **"The Board would consider"** — hypothetical; creates no obligation. Replace with SHALL consider.
- **"Counterparty can request"** — "can" implies ability, not right. Use MAY for permissions.

---

## DPDP Act 2023 Note

Section 7 consent obligations: data principals **SHALL** be notified (mandatory). Use SHALL for all data processor obligations under the Act.
